Project 1

Shop Goodreads on Amazon

Helping customers buy their next read

Overview

The Project

I designed a highly-requested feature that brings users' Goodreads Want to Read shelf into Amazon Your Books, letting them browse, filter, and shop their WTR titles all in one place.

Role

Primary UX Designer

Timeline

4 months; Launched

Team

Kindle & Goodreads PM, UX, Engineering

Problem

A disconnected shopping experience

Readers use Goodreads to track books they want to read, but this information isn't easily accessible when they shop on Amazon.

  • Users struggle to find books already saved to their Want to Read shelf
  • 41.7M linked Goodreads and Amazon customers
  • Opportunity to integrate Goodreads into Amazon for seamless shopping
Design Challenge

How might we reduce friction between tracking books on Goodreads and purchasing them on Amazon?

The Challenge

Balancing integration with trust

Data showed the separation between Goodreads and Amazon was intentional:

  • Only one-third of linked users maintain lists on both platforms
  • Just one-fifth of books overlap between platforms
  • Users see Goodreads as impartial discovery, Amazon as transactional

Central design challenge: reducing friction through integration without undermining the trust that makes Goodreads valuable.

Mission

Design Principles

Design Do's

  • Convenience: Single source of truth for total reading life
  • Trust: Control visibility of books on both platforms
  • Transparency: Context for where/why they see Goodreads content

Design Don'ts

  • Friction: No context-switching between apps
  • Bias: No sponsored content in Goodreads recommendations
Design Decision #1

Information Architecture

Where does Goodreads fit into Your Books?

  1. Add Goodreads WTR as a filter and card
  2. Add Goodreads as a secondary tab
  3. Add Goodreads as a primary tab

Decision: Fully integrate into existing Your Books experience rather than a separate tab—builds on existing mental models, avoids fragmenting the experience.

Design Decision #2

Badging

How do we differentiate Amazon wishlists from Goodreads shelves?

Validating with users

Ran unmoderated usability test with 8 users on 3 badging options:

Result: Clear preference for "On Goodreads shelf" label due to clarity and recognizability.

Final Badging
Solution

Introducing a new integration

Members who have linked their Amazon and Goodreads accounts can now see books from their Want to Read shelf in Amazon Your Books.

See all your lists in one place

Users can view their Goodreads Want to Read shelf alongside Amazon wishlists for consolidated tracking and shopping.

Shop all your books at once

Browse complete book collection from both Amazon and Goodreads in one unified view.

Your books. Your choice.

Users have full control to hide Goodreads books or unlink accounts entirely.

Outcome

Impact

Launched April 22, 2025 for 9.2M linked monthly active users (41.7M total).

86% higher engagement

WTR shelved books vs. Wishlist books

Customer Feedback

Project 2

Color Writing on Kindle

Designing color writing and annotation for Kindle Scribe's debut e-ink color display

Overview

The Project

I owned the design for the color writing and annotation experience for Kindle Scribe's debut e-ink color display.

Role

UX Designer

Timeline

7 months; Launched Dec 2025

Team

Kindle UX, PM, Engineering

Opportunity

Kindle's first color writing device

Kindle Color Scribe introduces color as a new medium for annotation, writing, and organization.

I had an opportunity to define what color means on a device built around focus and simplicity: enabling richer ways to highlight, write, and structure ideas without compromising the calm, immersive reading experience Kindle is known for.

Design Challenge

How might we introduce powerful color writing tools while still preserving the flow of deep reading and writing?

Design Challenge #1

Adding color to annotations

The in-book contextual action bar (CAB) had to feel almost invisible—powerful when needed, effortless when not.

Challenge: Fitting significantly more functionality (multiple annotation types and colors) into a space that was already constrained and frequently used.

Iterating on the CAB

Early explorations tested multiple interaction models: toggles, nested menus, explicit delete actions.

Simplifying the menu

Key decisions:

  • Removed explicit delete action—folded into "None" option in color submenu
  • Kept highlight and underline as first-class actions (not nested)
  • Prioritized clarity for today's core use cases

Designing cross-surface

The CAB needed to flex across color and black-and-white devices with different technical constraints.

Approach: Surface-appropriate designs that preserved the same mental model while adapting interaction patterns.

Result

Final annotation experience

Fast, flexible annotation without interrupting reading flow.

CSAT: 6.14/7 for color highlights and underlines in beta testing

Design Challenge #2

Color switching friction

User research and beta feedback consistently surfaced color switching as a pain point.

Pen and highlighter color options were nested in a submenu, requiring multiple taps to switch color while writing.

Exploring solutions

Three primary directions:

  1. Color slots: Fastest switching, minimal cognitive load once learned
  2. Pen presets: Bundled tool type + color, but added setup complexity
  3. Floating palettes: Most discoverable, but covered writing space
Result

Color slots solution

Aligned on color slots as the best balance of speed, simplicity, and feasibility.

Customer satisfaction: 6.10/7 with over 80% rating pen color controls as easy or very easy to use.

Design Challenge #3

Templates

Rather than designing from taste, I partnered with UX Research to anchor decisions in user input.

What users actually wanted

Research revealed clear patterns:

  • Users favored simple, versatile templates over decorative designs
  • Contrary to internal pressure, users preferred black-and-white templates
  • Customizability emerged as the strongest unmet need

Making 30 templates manageable

As the library grew, discoverability became the next challenge.

Solution: "Recently used" row at the top of the picker—optimized for speed and learnability without extra taps.

93% rating for creating notebooks with templates—users reported it as "intuitive"

Solution

Writing that feels like pen and paper

Configurable color slots enable one-tap switching between most-used colors, while lasso select helps make quick bulk color adjustments.

Powerful annotation experience

Highlight, underline, and annotate in color. Color and date filters let you quickly find what matters most.

Curated templates

Added 12 new, top-ranked templates based on user research, plus familiar colored options.

Outcome

Launch

Kindle Scribe Colorsoft launched December 10, 2025.

Press

What reviewers are saying

Learnings

What I learned

  • Define new interaction patterns while earning user trust on a traditionally grayscale product
  • Systems thinking across surfaces—when to unify vs. allow divergence
  • Reduce cognitive load and preserve flow in high-speed interactions
  • Treat technical constraints as design inputs—adapting solutions to remain intuitive
